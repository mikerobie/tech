Here's What I've Noticed

Most HR technology is built by people
who've never done HR.

And most HR people have no way to build the tools they actually need. I sit right in the middle of that gap. Here's what that looks like in the real world:

01

The spreadsheet that runs everything

Somewhere in your org, there's a mission-critical Excel file held together by VLOOKUP and prayers. Nobody wants to touch it. Everybody depends on it. That's a tool problem disguised as a people problem.

02

Compliance checks done by memory

ICTAP clearance, FLSA classification, JOA requirements — the knowledge lives in somebody's head. When that person's out, the process stops. When that person leaves, the knowledge walks out the door.

03

Hiring workflows built for 2009

Multiple approval layers, manual cert tracking, assessment processes that screen out good candidates. The role stays open for months. The team stays stretched. Everybody blames "the system."

04

"We need an app for that"

You know exactly what tool would fix the problem. You can describe every screen. But you can't build it, IT has a two-year backlog, and the vendor solution costs $80K/year and does half of what you need.

05

Good ideas stuck in slide decks

You pitched it. Leadership liked it. Then it went into the "future initiatives" pile, which is where good ideas go to collect dust. What it needed was a working prototype, not another presentation.

06

AI hype, zero execution

Everyone's talking about AI transforming HR. Nobody's actually building anything. The gap isn't knowledge — it's someone who understands both the domain and the tech well enough to make something real.

How I Work

From napkin sketch to working product.
Here's how it goes.

I keep it simple. Identify the problem. Build the smallest thing that solves it. Iterate from there.

Step 01

Find the Real Problem

Not the symptom. Not the thing somebody put in a Jira ticket. The actual root cause — the thing that, if you fixed it, would make three other problems disappear.

Step 02

Build the Ugly Version

Get something working fast. Prove the concept. Show it to a real user on the first day if possible. Pretty comes later. Functional comes now.

Step 03

Iterate With Feedback

Put it in front of the people who'll actually use it. Watch what they do. Listen to what they say. Rebuild the parts that don't work. Cut the parts nobody touches.

Step 04

Ship It Clean

Documented. Deployable. Built so the next person can pick it up without a three-hour walkthrough. If it can't survive without me, it's not done yet.
